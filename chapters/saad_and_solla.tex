\section{The Saad \& Solla equations}
We now turn to the result from the literature on which much of this thesis is built.
Compressing the problem to the order parameters $(Q, M, \vec{a})$ is only half of the
story; the other half is the \emph{high-dimensional limit} $d \to \infty$. In this
regime the stochastic fluctuations of the order parameters around their mean become
negligible, and the entire training trajectory concentrates on a deterministic path
governed by a system of \emph{ordinary differential equations} (ODEs).

\subsection{The order-parameter dynamics}
We can make Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:sgd} explicit in terms of the local fields, and write the stochastic dynamics of the weights $\vec{w}_j$ and the output weights $a_j$ of the student network as
\begin{align}
    \vec{w}_j^{\nu+1} &= \vec{w}_j^{\nu} + \frac{\gamma}{p\sqrt{d}} \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) a_j \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \vec{x}^\nu, \\
    %
    a_j^{\nu+1} &= a_j^{\nu} + \frac{\gamma}{pd} \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) \sigma(\lambda^\nu_j)
\end{align}
where \(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}(\langle \vec{w}_1, \vec{x}^\nu \rangle, \ldots, \langle \vec{w}_p, \vec{x}^\nu \rangle)\) and \(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}(\langle \vec{w}_1^\star, \vec{x}^\nu \rangle, \ldots, \langle \vec{w}_k^\star, \vec{x}^\nu \rangle)\) are the \emph{local fields} (i.e., pre-activations) of the student and teacher networks, respectively, relative to the current input $\vec{x}^\nu$ used in the $\nu$-th SGD step. The quantity \(y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) \coloneqq y(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) + \sqrt{\Delta}\xi^\nu\) is the noisy label of the current training sample.
The second-layer weights $a_j$ are updated with a rescaled learning rate $\gamma/d$, which keeps both layers evolving on the same timescale as $d \to \infty$.
With a little extra work we can derive the update rule for the order parameters themselves,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:op-update}
\begin{aligned}
    Q^{\nu+1}_{jl} =& Q^{\nu}_{jl} + \frac{\gamma}{pd} \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) \left( a_l \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_l) \lambda^\nu_j + a_j \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \lambda^\nu_l  \right) + \\ & + \frac{\gamma}{p d} \frac{\gamma}{p}\left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right)^2 a_j a_l \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_l) \frac{\norm{\vec{x}^\nu}^2}{d}, \\
    M^{\nu+1}_{jr} =& M^{\nu}_{jr} + \frac{\gamma}{pd} \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) a_j \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \lambda^{\star,\nu}_r , \\
    a_j^{\nu+1} =& a_j^{\nu} + \frac{\gamma}{pd} \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) \sigma(\lambda^\nu_j).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Since we are interested in the high-dimensional limit, the factor $\sfrac{\norm{\vec{x}^\nu}^2}{d}$ in the $Q$ update can be replaced by its expectation $\mathbb{E}\left[\sfrac{\norm{\vec{x}^\nu}^2}{d}\right] = 1$. \addnote{This is the classical \emph{à la physique} argument from \citet{saad1995exact}. In the next section we will report a formalization of this argument.}
The update rules above are still stochastic, but they no longer depend explicitly on the input $\vec{x}^\nu$: they involve only the local fields $\vec{\lambda}^\nu$ and $\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}$, which are Gaussian random variables whose covariance is given by the order parameters themselves. In other words, we have a \emph{duality} between the process in the space of the weights \(W\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times d}\) and the process in the space of the order parameters \((Q, M, \vec{a})\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times p}\times\mathbb{R}^{p\times k}\times\mathbb{R}^p\)
\begin{align*}
    \text{update rule for } W \quad &\leftrightarrow \quad \text{update rule for } (Q, M, \vec{a}) \\
    \text{sample } \vec{x}^\nu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d) \quad &\leftrightarrow \quad \text{sample } (\vec{\lambda}^\nu, \vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega) \\
    \text{population risk } \mathcal{R}(W) \quad &\leftrightarrow \quad \text{population risk } \mathcal{R}(Q, M, \vec{a}).
\end{align*}
This duality gives a full characterization of the random SGD process in dimension \(d\) that is itself independent of \(d\). The population risk is a quantity that fully characterizes the generalization properties of the student network, and it can be computed directly from the order parameters; the dynamics, in turn, is self-contained in the order-parameter space, so we can study the generalization of the student without ever dealing with the high-dimensional weights \(W\) directly.

\subsection{From discrete updates to ODEs}
The update rules in Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:op-update} can be rearranged as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \frac{Q^{\nu+1}_{jl}-Q^{\nu}_{jl}}{\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}} =& \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) \left( a_l \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_l) \lambda^\nu_j + a_j \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \lambda^\nu_l  \right) + \\ & + \frac{\gamma}{p}\left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right)^2 a_j a_l \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j) \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_l), \\
    \frac{M^{\nu+1}_{jr}-M^{\nu}_{jr}}{\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}} =& \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) a_j \sigma'(\lambda^\nu_j), \\
    \frac{a_j^{\nu+1}-a_j^{\nu}}{\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}} =& \left( y^\nu(\vec{\lambda}^{\star,\nu}) - h(\vec{\lambda}^{\nu}) \right) \sigma(\lambda^\nu_j).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Intuitively, when \(d\to\infty\) the left-hand side of the equations above can be interpreted as a derivative with respect to a continuous time variable \(t = \nu \frac{\gamma}{pd}\), while the right-hand side can be replaced by its expectation over the Gaussian distribution of the local fields, which is parametrized by the order parameters themselves. \addnote{In the traditional derivation of the Saad \& Solla equations, the usual time scaling is $t=\frac{\nu}{d}$. We will later clarify why we prefer this scaling.} This leads to a system of ODEs for the order parameters, known as the \emph{Saad \& Solla equations}~\cite{saad1995exact,saad1996dynamics}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:ss-odes} \tag{SS-ODEs}
\begin{aligned}
    \dod{Q_{jl}}{t} =& \overbrace{\mathbb{E}_{(\vec{\lambda}, \vec{\lambda}^\star) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega)}
    \left[ \left( h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^{\star}) - h(\vec{\lambda}) \right) \left( a_l \sigma'(\lambda_l) \lambda_j + a_j \sigma'(\lambda_j) \lambda_l  \right)\right]}^{\textstyle \eqqcolon \Phi^{Q}_{jl}(\Omega, a)}\\ & + \frac{\gamma}{p} \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{(\vec{\lambda}, \vec{\lambda}^\star) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega)}\left[ \left(\left( h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^{\star}) - h(\vec{\lambda}) \right)^2+\Delta\right) a_j a_l \sigma'(\lambda_j) \sigma'(\lambda_l) \right]}_{\textstyle \eqqcolon \Psi^{Q}_{jl}(\Omega, a)}, \\[0.9em]
    \dod{M_{jr}}{t} =& \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{(\vec{\lambda}, \vec{\lambda}^\star) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega)} \left[ \left( h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^{\star}) - h(\vec{\lambda}) \right) a_j \sigma'(\lambda_j) \lambda^{\star}_r \right]}_{\textstyle \eqqcolon \Phi^{M}_{jr}(\Omega, a)}, \\[0.9em]
    \dod{a_j}{t} =& \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{(\vec{\lambda}, \vec{\lambda}^\star) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega)} \left[ \left( h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^{\star}) - h(\vec{\lambda}) \right) \sigma(\lambda_j) \right]}_{\textstyle \eqqcolon \Phi^{a}_{j}(\Omega, a)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Once the initial conditions are fixed, the trajectory drawn by these equations is completely determined: the dynamics is deterministic, and the randomness of the training process has been averaged out in the high-dimensional limit.
\begin{remark}
    Note that, even though the Saad \& Solla equations determine a deterministic trajectory for the order parameters, the actual trajectory of the weights $W$ is still stochastic, and impossible to characterize without knowing the specific realization of the training data \(\vec{x}^\nu\). The Saad \& Solla equations only describe the trajectory of the order parameters, and consequently of the population risk.
\end{remark}

The formalization of the argument above, together with the proof of convergence of the stochastic process to the deterministic one, was first given by \citet{goldt2019dynamics}. \addnote{The original theorem was stated for matching student and teacher networks, but the proof extends straightforwardly to the more general case of a multi-index teacher and a general student network.}
\begin{theorem}[Convergence of the Saad \& Solla equations, \cite{goldt2019dynamics}] \label{thm:intro:ss-convergence}
    Suppose that
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item The function \(\sigma\) is bounded, and its derivatives up to the second order exist and are bounded too;
    \item The function \(h^\star\) grows at most like a polynomial, i.e., \(|h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^\star)| \le K(1 + \|\vec{\lambda}^\star\|^\alpha)\) for some constants \(K\) and \(\alpha\);
    \item \(W^\star\) is of rank \(k\);
    \item The initial macroscopic state \({\Omega^0}\) is deterministic and bounded by a constant;
    \item The constants \(\Delta, p, k\) and \(\gamma\) are all finite.
  \end{enumerate}
  Then, for any \(T>0\), the overlap matrix satisfies
  \begin{equation}
    \max_{0\le\nu\le Td} \E\left[
      \left\|({\Omega}^\nu,a^\nu) - \left(\tilde{{\Omega}}\left(\frac{\nu\gamma}{pd}\right), \tilde{a}\left(\frac{\nu\gamma}{pd}\right)\right)\right\|
    \right]
    \le
    \frac{C{(T)}}{\sqrt{d}},
  \end{equation}
  where \(\left(\tilde{{\Omega}}, \tilde{a}\right)\) is the unique solution of Equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes},
  with the initial condition \[\left(\tilde{{\Omega}}{(0)}, \tilde{a{(0)}}\right) = \left(\Omega^0, a^0\right).\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Anatomy of the equations: learning vs.\ noise}
It is worth interpreting the actual content of the Saad \& Solla equations; for this we follow the nomenclature used by \citet{veiga2022phase}. There are two types of terms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{learning terms} \(\Phi\): these are the terms that drive the dynamics to align the student with the teacher, and consequently to reduce the population risk; it can be shown that these same terms are the only ones that survive in the \emph{gradient flow} limit, and are therefore what drives the learning.
    \item \emph{variance term} \(\Psi\): this is the term that arises from the stochasticity of the training process, and it is responsible for the fluctuations of the order parameters around their mean trajectory; notably, it is proportional to the learning rate \(\gamma\) and does \emph{not} vanish for \(\Delta = 0\). This is the crucial noise introduced by SGD, and is precisely what differentiates it from the gradient flow limit.
\end{enumerate}
When label noise is present (\(\Delta>0\)), the irreducible \(\Delta\)-dependent contribution to the variance term is responsible for the \emph{noise plateau} phenomenon \cite{veiga2022phase}: the order parameters converge to a suboptimal fixed point and cannot reach the global minimum even as \(t\to\infty\); see Figure~\ref{intro:fig:plateau}.

Practitioners often address this problem with a decaying learning rate, which effectively makes the variance term vanish, combined with larger batch sizes, which reduce the variance of the stochastic gradients by averaging out the label noise.
It is natural to ask how Equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} change when the learning rate and the batch size are themselves allowed to scale with the dimension \(d\); this is exactly the topic of Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2406}. Letting both scale as powers of \(d\) modifies the Saad \& Solla equations through \emph{intra-batch}, finite-\(d\) corrections to the otherwise dimension-free dynamics, and uncovers a genuine \emph{time/complexity trade-off}: a larger batch averages the gradient noise and suppresses the variance term \(\Psi\), but beyond a critical size it no longer reduces the number of steps and merely consumes more samples per step. The resulting phase diagram, in the \(n_b\)--\(\gamma\) plane of the batch size and the learning rate, separates a regime in which the network fails to learn, an intermediate regime on the edge of learning where the plateau persists, and a regime of successful recovery, and it pinpoints the batch size that minimizes the training time.

\subsection{Closed-form examples}
Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} can fully describe the dynamics only when it is actually possible to integrate analytically the expectations on the right-hand side, arriving at a closed-form expression for \(\Phi\) and \(\Psi\) as functions of the order parameters. This depends strongly on the choice of the activation function \(\sigma\) and of the teacher function \(h^\star\); in the literature the problem is often tackled in the simple setting of \emph{matching architectures}.

Assume for simplicity that the \emph{teacher} is itself a two-layer network, with $k$ hidden neurons and the same activation function $\sigma$ as the \emph{student} (which has $p$ hidden neurons):
\begin{equation}
    f^\star(\vec{x}) = \frac1k \sum_{j=1}^k a_j^\star \sigma\left(\frac{\vec{w}_j^\star \cdot \vec{x}}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
    \quad\text{or equivalently}\quad
    h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^\star) = \frac1k \sum_{j=1}^k a_j^\star \sigma(\lambda_j^\star)
\end{equation}
The integrability of Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} then boils down to whether we can compute, for a generic covariance \(C_s \in PSD\mathbb{R}^{p \times p}\), the following expectations:
\begin{align}
    I_2^\text{risk}(C_2) &\coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{(z_1, z_2) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, C_2)}\left[ \sigma(z_1) \sigma(z_2) \right], \\
    I_2^\text{noise}(C_2) &\coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{(z_1, z_2) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, C_2)}\left[ \sigma'(z_1) \sigma'(z_2) \right], \\
    I_3(C_3) &\coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{(z_1, z_2, z_3) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, C_3)}\left[ \sigma(z_1) \sigma'(z_2) z_3 \right], \\
    I_4(C_4) &\coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{(z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, C_4)}\left[ \sigma'(z_1) \sigma'(z_2) \sigma(z_3) \sigma(z_4) \right].
\end{align}
Here \(I_2^\text{risk}\) appears in the expression of the population risk and \(\Phi^a\), \(I_3\) appears in the learning terms \(\Phi^M\) and \(\Phi^Q\), while \(I_2^\text{noise}\) and \(I_4\) appear in the variance term \(\Psi^Q\).

A substantial body of literature has been devoted to whether these expectations admit a closed form, and hence to whether the asymptotic equations are exact, depending on the choice of the activation function \(\sigma\). \addnote{Some of these activations do not fully satisfy the assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:intro:ss-convergence}, yet they have been shown to work empirically; the theorem is, in all likelihood, not tight.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{linear} \(\sigma(x) = x\): this case is trivial, since the student and the teacher are then just linear models, and the dynamics can be fully solved in closed form;
    \item \emph{error function} \(\sigma(x) = \erf(\sfrac{x}{\sqrt{2}})\): this is the original setting of \citet{saad1995exact}, where the equations can be fully solved in closed form and the dynamics fully characterized;
    \item \emph{ReLU} \(\sigma(x) = \max(0, x)\): this is the most common activation function used in practice, but it has no closed-form solution for the expectation \(I_4\). The other integrals were studied in \citet{yoshida2019data} using results from \citet{yoshida2017statistical};
    \item \emph{phase retrieval} \(\sigma(x) = x^2\): the study of Equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} for this activation function is the full content of Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305}. We determined the exact closed form of the expectations and used it to study the number of samples needed to learn the teacher network;
    \item \emph{polynomial} \(\sigma(x) = p(x)\): the framework introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305} can be extended to any polynomial activation function. The code attached to that work can be used to compute the exact closed form of the expectations, although the number of terms grows exponentially with the degree of the polynomial, making it impractical for high-degree polynomials;
    \item \emph{GeLU} \(\sigma(x) = x \Phi(x)\): this is a smooth approximation of the ReLU function, and it has been shown to admit a closed-form solution for the expectations \(I_2\) and \(I_3\) \cite{richert2023layered}.
\end{itemize}
The other activations commonly used in practice (such as sigmoid, \(\tanh\), and so on) do not admit a closed-form solution for the expectations, and the dynamics can then only be studied numerically, by approximating the expectations.
One option is a Monte Carlo approximation of the expectations, but in our experience this is rarely precise enough and often leads to worse results than simulating SGD on the weights directly. A better option is to approximate the activation function with Hermite polynomials, for which the expectations do admit a closed form;
\addnote{Expanding in Hermite polynomials along every dimension is essentially equivalent to a tensor-product Gauss--Hermite quadrature of the correlated expectation; this technique is used in Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2506}, in a slightly different setting.}
a more advanced technique for approximating the expectations is discussed in \citet{citton2024line}, where the authors expand both the activation function and the correlated Gaussian measure in the Hermite basis to obtain a very precise approximation of the expectations for any activation function.

Even though the Saad \& Solla equations are not always analytically solvable, they remain a very powerful tool for studying the dynamics of SGD around initialization: for small displacements, any \(\sigma\) and \(h^\star\) can be expanded in a series of Hermite polynomials, which again yields a closed-form expression for the expectations and approximates the dynamics of the order parameters. This idea is at the core of several chapters of this thesis, and we develop it further in the next section.


\subsection{Beyond the high-dimensional limit}\label{sec:intro:beyond}
When deriving Equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes}, we took the infinitesimal time step to be \(\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}\), which indeed vanishes as \(d\to\infty\). However, this is not the only way to obtain a vanishing time step:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(\gamma \to 0\), \(d,p\) finite: this is the classical limit that, for online SGD, converges to \emph{gradient flow} \cite{robbins1951stochastic};
    \item \(p \to \infty\), \(d, \gamma\) finite: this is the infinite-width limit, sometimes called the \emph{mean-field} limit, studied for two-layer networks in \cite{mei2018mean, chizat2018global,rotskoff2022trainability,sirignano2020mean}.
\end{itemize}
The key observation is that the convergence of the overlap process to these equations is, more generally, controlled by the effective time step \(\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}\) alone: a non-asymptotic refinement of Theorem~\ref{thm:intro:ss-convergence} due to \citet{veiga2022phase} bounds the deviation from the deterministic trajectory by a quantity that vanishes whenever \(\sfrac{\gamma}{pd}\to 0^+\), regardless of how that limit is reached. The deterministic, low-dimensional description in terms of the order parameters is therefore valid \emph{beyond} the high-dimensional limit on which the original Saad \& Solla analysis focused, and the three limits above can be treated on the same footing. This equivalence holds, however, only on finite time horizons \(t = \mathcal{O}(\log d)\): the bound of \citet{veiga2022phase} carries a prefactor that grows exponentially in time, so it controls the \(\mathcal{O}(1)\)-time regimes treated here but not the much longer timescales on which learning escapes its initial plateau. Those long-time results rest on separate stochastic arguments rather than on the deterministic ODE, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:intro:exponents} and quantified in Appendix~\ref{app:intro:validity-window}.

In Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2302} we build on this observation to study the connection between the Saad \& Solla equations and the other two limits within a single framework, summarized by the phase diagram of Figure~\ref{intro:fig:triangle}. Its three corners are the high-dimensional, classical gradient-flow, and mean-field limits, while the edges and interior cover combinations such as the \emph{high-dimensional gradient-flow} and \emph{high-dimensional mean-field} limits, which are not captured by the classical Saad \& Solla equations.

The mean-field corner is the most delicate. Because Equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} track the \(O(p^2 + pk)\) order parameters, they become intractable precisely in the overparametrized regime \(p\to\infty\) for which the mean-field description \cite{mei2018mean,chizat2018global,rotskoff2022trainability,sirignano2020mean} was devised. We bridge the two by exploiting the fact that, throughout training, the student weights remain close to the low-dimensional subspace spanned by the teacher directions: projecting the dynamics onto this subspace collapses the growing system of ODEs back to a description of constant dimension, recovering a mean-field PDE while keeping the explicit dependence on \(d\). This connects the order-parameter picture of this section to the hydrodynamic, optimal-transport viewpoint of the mean-field literature.
