\section{Information exponent and beyond}
\label{sec:intro:exponents}
The core question of this section is to characterize the sample complexity of learning a function \(h^\star\), given an expressive enough student. We show that the answer is controlled by a single integer, the \emph{information exponent} of \citet{arous2021online}, which measures the flatness of the population risk at the initialization saddle. We then discuss two generalizations: the \emph{leap exponent} of \citet{abbe2022merged, abbe2023sgd}, which governs the multi-index case \(k > 1\), and the \emph{generative exponent} of \citet{damian2024computational}, which measures the hardness of the problem under a broader class of algorithms. 


We focus for now on the \emph{single-index} setting, where the teacher is a single-index model \(f^\star(\vec{x}) = h^\star(\vec{w}^\star \cdot \vec{x}/\sqrt{d})\) and the student has the same shape \(f(\vec{x}) = \sigma(\vec{w} \cdot \vec{x}/\sqrt{d})\) with a different activation function \(\sigma\). The overlap matrices of Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:overlaps} then collapse to two scalars, the \emph{self-overlap} and the \emph{teacher overlap},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intro:single-index-overlaps}
    q = \frac{\norm{\vec{w}}^2}{d}, \qquad m = \frac{\langle \vec{w}, \vec{w}^\star\rangle}{d},
\end{equation}
and the pre-activations are jointly Gaussian, \((\lambda, \lambda^\star) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Omega)\) with covariance \[\Omega = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} q & m \\ m & 1\end{smallmatrix}\right).\]
We make one more assumption on top of the setting of Section~\ref{intro:sec:settings}:
\begin{assumption}[Projected SGD]
    The student is trained with projected SGD, where each step is projected back onto the sphere \(\mathbb{S}^{d-1}(\sqrt{d})\); in other words, the update rule
    \[\vec{w}^{\tau+1} = \sqrt{d}\,\frac{\vec{w}^{\tau} - \gamma \nabla_{\vec{w}}\ell(f(\vec{x}^{\tau}), y^{\tau})}{\norm{\vec{w}^{\tau} - \gamma \nabla_{\vec{w}}\ell(f(\vec{x}^{\tau}), y^{\tau})}}.\]
\end{assumption}
The effect of this is to keep the norm of the student weights fixed, \(q = 1\), so that the only degree of freedom is the teacher overlap \(m\).
This technical simplification lets us focus on the true signal --- the correlation with the teacher direction --- by decoupling it from changes in the student-weight norm, which can alter the risk without contributing to recovery; see Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305} for a detailed discussion.
The Saad \& Solla equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} then reduce to a single ODE for \(m\) that naturally encodes the spherical constraint
\addnote{We are actually neglecting the \emph{variance term} \(\Psi\) in the self-overlap equation, which is subleading in the small-learning-rate regime that governs the escape from initialization; in Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305} we keep it and show that the derivation goes through.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere}
\begin{aligned}
    \dod{m}{t} =& \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[ \left( h^\star(\lambda^\star) - \sigma(\lambda) \right) \sigma'(\lambda)\,\lambda^\star \right]}_{\text{drift}}
                -m\underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[ \left( h^\star(\lambda^\star) - \sigma(\lambda) \right) \sigma'(\lambda)\,\lambda \right]}_{\text{spherical constraint}}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The equation above can be rearranged in a more compact and insightful form
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact}
    \dod{m}{t} = (1 - m^2)\,C'(m) \quad \text{with } C(m) = \mathbb{E}[h^\star(\lambda^\star)\,\sigma(\lambda)].
\end{equation}
The derivation is reported in Appendix~\ref{app:intro:m-ode-sphere}. The prefactor \((1 - m^2)\) is purely \emph{geometric}: the spherical Jacobian \(\sin^2\theta\) for \(m = \cos\theta\). Hence the activations \(\sigma\) and \(h^\star\) enter the dynamics only through \(C(m)\), and the equation is exact for every \(m \in [-1, 1]\), not merely near initialization. On the sphere the marginal terms of \(\mathcal{R}\) are frozen, leaving
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:correlation-potential}
    \mathcal{R}(m) = \mathrm{const} - C(m),
\end{equation}
so that the overlap dynamics is gradient ascent on \(C\).

Since one-pass SGD consumes one sample per gradient step, the sample complexity equals
\[n = \frac{d}{\gamma}\,t,\]
so the time \(t\) serves as a direct proxy for the number of samples up to the constant \(d/\gamma\).
Although the ODE~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact} is dimension-independent, its initial condition is not: at random initialization \(\vec{w}^0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)\), the student-teacher overlap is a sum of \(d\) independent mean-zero terms, and is therefore tiny,
\[m^0 = \frac{\langle \vec{w}^0, \vec{w}^\star \rangle}{d} = \mathcal{O}_d\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}\right).\]
The student starts nearly orthogonal to the teacher, and the dynamics must drag \(m\) from \(\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{d})\) up to a finite, \(d\)-independent value before any meaningful alignment is established. This is the bottleneck that determines the \(d\)-scaling of the total sample count; once \(m\) reaches a finite value, the remaining optimization is fast and contributes only sub-leading terms. The relevant milestone is therefore \emph{weak recovery}.
\begin{definition}[Weak recovery]
    We say that the student has achieved weak recovery of the teacher with threshold \(\eta\) if the teacher overlap \(m\) has reached a finite value \(\eta\) independent of the dimension \(d\):
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:weak-recovery}
        m = \frac{\langle \vec{w}, \vec{w}^\star \rangle}{d} \geq \eta, \qquad \eta \in (0, 1).
    \end{equation}
    We denote by \(t_\text{weak}(\eta)\) the first time at which the solution to Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact} reaches \(m(t_\text{weak}) = \eta\).
\end{definition}
The sample complexity of weak recovery is therefore \(n = \mathcal{O}_d(d\,t_\text{weak}(\eta))\).

In practice, we can take \(\eta\) small enough that Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact} is well approximated by its leading order expansion around \(m = 0\)
\addnote{The actual factor in front of \(m^{\ell-1}\) is not dependent on \(d\) and does not affect the final escaping complexity. In the same way, the factor \((1 - m^2)\) does not change the leading order of the expansion.}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intro:approx-m-ode}
    \dod{m}{t} \propto C^{(\ell)}(0)\,m^{\ell-1}, \qquad \ell = \min\{j \geq 1 : C^{(j)}(0) \neq 0\}.
\end{equation}
We can better understand the meaning of this by looking at some examples.
\begin{example}[Linear regression]
    For the linear regression target \(h^\star(x) = x\) and the linear activation \(\sigma(x) = x\), the correlation potential is \(C(m) = m\), so \(C'(m) = 1\) and
    \begin{equation}
        \dod{m}{t} = 1 - m^2 \overset{m\ll 1}{\approx} 1\quad \text{with i.c. } m^0 \approx 1/\sqrt{d}.
    \end{equation}
    The solution is \(m(t) = m^0 + t\), so the weak recovery time is \(t_\text{weak}(\eta) \approx \eta\), giving a total sample complexity of \(n = \mathcal{O}_d(d)\).
    % TODO: cite result for linear regression.
\end{example}
\begin{example}[Phase retrieval]
    For the phase retrieval target \(h^\star(x) = x^2\) and the quadratic activation \(\sigma(x) = x^2\), the correlation potential is \(C(m) = 1 + 2m^2\), so \(C'(m) = 4m\) and
    \begin{equation}
        \dod{m}{t} = 4\,m\,(1 - m^2) \overset{m\ll 1}{\approx} 4\,m\quad \text{with i.c. } m^0 \approx 1/\sqrt{d}.
    \end{equation}
    The solution is \(m(t) = m^0\,e^{4t}\), so the weak recovery time is \(t_\text{weak}(\eta) \approx \frac{1}{4}\log(\eta\sqrt{d})\), giving a total sample complexity of \(n = \mathcal{O}_d(d\log d)\).
    \addnote{\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes} do not concentrate for infinitesimally small \(m\), so to be rigorous one should take into account stochastic correction; we do this in Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305} and show that the scaling is unchanged.}
    In Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2305} we carry out a finer analysis of the equation, studying the precise constants and how they scale with the learning rate \(\gamma\) and the label noise \(\Delta\).
\end{example}

In summary: the flatter the correlation potential (equivalently, the population risk) at initialization, the harder the escape from the saddle at \(m = 0\), and the more samples needed to achieve weak recovery. Geometrically, in weight space, the population risk at \(\vec{w}^0\) is a \emph{strict saddle}, flat along all but one direction---a golf course with a single hole---so the student is stuck at \emph{mediocrity}, only weakly correlated with the teacher. In the space of the order parameter \(m\), this translates into a flat potential \(C(m)\) at \(m = 0\) (Figure~\ref{fig:intro:correlation-potentials}), with initialization very close to the critical point.

The integer \(\ell\) in Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:approx-m-ode} is the \emph{information exponent} of \citet{arous2021online}, and it is the only quantity that matters for the sample complexity of learning a single-index target with SGD. Expanding \(h^\star = \sum_k a_k \mathrm{He}_k\) and \(\sigma = \sum_k b_k \mathrm{He}_k\), and using the orthogonality of Hermite polynomials under a correlated Gaussian, \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{He}_j(\lambda)\mathrm{He}_k(\lambda^\star)] = \delta_{jk}\,k!\,m^k\); the correlation is then a pure power series in the overlap,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:correlation-series}
    C(m) = \sum_{k \geq 0} k!\,a_k\,b_k\, m^k,
\end{equation}
so that near the saddle \(\mathcal{R}(m) - \mathcal{R}(0) = -\sum_{k\geq1} k!\,a_k b_k\, m^k\).
\begin{assumption}[$\sigma$ excites the $\ell$-th mode]
    The student activation \(\sigma\) is such that \(b_\ell = \frac{1}{\ell!}\,\mathbb{E}[\sigma\,\mathrm{He}_\ell] \neq 0\).
    In other words, the student is expressive enough to excite the mode of any teacher.
\end{assumption}
The first term that bends the landscape is the lowest \(k\) with \(a_k \neq 0\)---which is exactly \(\ell\). In words, the information exponent is the order to which the population risk is flat at the mediocrity saddle: \(\mathcal{R}(m) - \mathcal{R}(0) \approx -\,\ell!\,a_\ell b_\ell\,m^\ell\). Near the saddle the geometric factor in~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact} is \(1 - m^2 \approx 1\), so the escape drift inherits the same order,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:drift-ie}
    \dod{m}{t} = (1-m^2)\,C'(m) \;\sim\; \ell\,\ell!\,a_\ell b_\ell\, m^{\ell - 1}.
\end{equation}
We can now state the original definition of the information exponent.
\begin{definition}[Information exponent \cite{arous2021online}]
    The information exponent \(\ell\) of a single-index target \(h^\star\) is the lowest order of the Hermite expansion of \(h^\star\) with non-zero coefficient,
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:intro:info-exponent}
        \ell = \min\{j \geq 1 : a_j = \frac{1}{j!}\,\mathbb{E}_{z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)}[h^\star(z)\,\mathrm{He}_j(z)] \neq 0\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The last piece of the puzzle is to understand how the information exponent controls the sample complexity of learning. Integrating the deterministic drift~\eqref{eq:intro:drift-ie} from \(m^0 = \mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{d})\) gives the escape time, hence the number of samples:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:info-scaling}
    t_\text{escape} \asymp \begin{cases}
        1 & \ell = 1, \\
        \log d & \ell = 2, \\
        d^{\ell/2-1} & \ell \geq 3.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
However, the escape time predicted by Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:drift-ie} is not sufficient to determine the sample complexity for \(\ell \geq 3\).
\addnote{To be precise, even for \(\ell = 2\) the stochastic noise dominates the escape time, but the drift is still enough to determine the scaling. Appendix~\ref{app:intro:validity-window} makes precise the time horizon over which the deterministic description is valid, and where instead these fluctuations take over.}
The deterministic drift captured by the ODE is only the mean of the stochastic dynamics, and the potential is so flat that the fluctuations of the order parameter around the mean trajectory cannot be neglected. Effectively, the dynamics can be divided in two parts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{search phase}: the student is stuck in the flat region around \(m = 0\), and the dynamics is dominated by the stochastic fluctuations of the order parameter; the escape time is therefore longer than predicted by the deterministic ODE~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact};
    \item \emph{descent phase}: once the student has escaped the flat region, the drift dominates and the dynamics is well approximated by the ODE~\eqref{eq:intro:m-ode-sphere-compact}; the escape time is therefore well captured by Saad \& Solla's equations~\eqref{eq:intro:ss-odes}.
\end{itemize}
The \emph{search phase} is the bottleneck that determines the scaling of the sample complexity with \(d\), while the \emph{descent phase} always happens linearly in \(d\) and contributes only sub-leading terms for \(\ell \geq 2\). 
The formal result requires more sophisticated tools, but the scaling is captured by the following theorem.
\addnote{Every information exponent \(\ell \ge 2\) hides an extra polylogarithmic factor, present but not proven sharp. Informally we quote the sharp bound; the formal proofs keep all logarithmic factors, a convention we follow throughout the thesis.}
\begin{theorem}[Informal version of \citet{arous2021online}]
    The sample complexity of learning a single-index target with information exponent \(\ell\) with one-pass SGD is
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:sgd-sample-complexity}
        n = \begin{cases}
            \mathcal{O}(d) & \ell = 1, \\
            \mathcal{O}(d\log d) & \ell = 2, \\
            \mathcal{O}(d^{\ell - 1}) & \ell \geq 3.
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The concept of information exponent is actually more general than one presented here. Every problem where the loss function depends only on the scalar product \(\langle \vec{w}, \vec{x}\rangle\), and it is optimized by one-pass spherical SGD, has a well-defined information exponent that controls the sample complexity of learning. This includes, for example, online PCA or spiked matrix/tensor estimation, or Gaussian mixture clustering, as in the seminal work of \citet{arous2021online}.

Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2406} extends the exponent to the single-index case where \(n_b\) and \(\gamma\) scale with \(d\).
The chapter reveals a sharp threshold at \(n_b \sim d^{\ell/2}\): below it, larger mini-batches reduce the number of gradient steps proportionally while keeping the total sample complexity \(N = n_b T = \mathcal{O}(d^{\ell-1})\) unchanged, offering a practical speed-up through parallelism; above it, the gradient signal is dominated by self-interaction terms and standard SGD fails to achieve weak recovery altogether.
Failing to scale the learning rate appropriately at large \(n_b\) degrades the sample complexity: there is only a curve in the space of \((n_b, \gamma)\) that achieves weak recovery with the optimal sample complexity.

Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2506} extends the exponent to a model that proxies the attention layer. The input \(X\in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}\) is now a sequence of \(L\) \(d\)-dimensional vectors, and our learned model is
\addnote{The bilinear read-out is displayed here for concreteness. Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2506} establishes its results for a general \emph{reduction map} \(\Pi : \mathbb{R}^{L\times L}\to\mathbb{R}^k\).}
\[f(X) = \vec{a}_L^\top \SoftMax\left(\left(X+\frac{P}{\sqrt{d}}\right) \vec{w}\vec{w}^\top \left(X+\frac{P}{\sqrt{d}}\right)^\top\right)\vec{a}_R,\]
where \(P\) is a fixed positional encoding, and \(\vec{a}_L, \vec{a}_R \in \mathbb{R}^L\) are the read-out vectors.
The information exponent is then defined as the smallest degree of the Hermite multivariate expansion of the target function, now called the \emph{Sequence Information Exponent} (SIE), and the sample complexity obeys the same \(d\)-scaling hierarchy as~\eqref{eq:intro:sgd-sample-complexity} with \(\ell\) replaced by the SIE.
The order-parameter space expands from the scalar \(m\) to the pair \((m, \vec{e})\), where \(m = \vec{w}^\star\cdot\vec{w}/d\) is the teacher overlap and \(\vec{e} = P\vec{w}/\sqrt{d}\) encodes the alignment of the student weights with the positional encoding \(P\); the dynamics is described by a coupled ODE in \((m,\vec{e})\), illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:intro:2506-trajectories}.

\subsection{The leap exponent}
When the teacher has more than one relevant direction (\(k > 1\)), the single-index picture extends naturally. The teacher is now a \emph{multi-index model},
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:multi-index-teacher}
    f^\star(\vec{x}) = h^\star\!\left(\frac{W^\star \vec{x}}{\sqrt{d}}\right), \qquad W^\star = (\vec{w}^\star_1, \ldots, \vec{w}^\star_k)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d},
\end{equation}
and the single overlap \(m\) of Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:single-index-overlaps} broadens to an overlap vector \(\vec{m} \in \mathbb{R}^k\),
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:multi-index-overlaps}
    m_r = \frac{\langle \vec{w}, \vec{w}^\star_r \rangle}{d}, \qquad r \in [k].
\end{equation}
The multivariate target \(h^\star : \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}\) admits a Hermite expansion in the teacher pre-activations \(\vec{\lambda}^\star = W^\star \vec{x}/\sqrt{d}\),
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:multi-index-hermite}
    h^\star(\vec{\lambda}) = \sum_{\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^k} a_\alpha \prod_{r=1}^k \mathrm{He}_{\alpha_r}(\lambda_r), \qquad a_\alpha = \frac{1}{\alpha!}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[h^\star(\vec{\lambda}^\star)\prod_{r=1}^k\mathrm{He}_{\alpha_r}(\lambda^\star_r)\right].
\end{equation}
Lifting the degree \(\ell\) part to the ambient space \(\mathbb{R}^d\) gives the \emph{Hermite tensor} of order \(\ell\),
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:hermite-tensor}
    T_\ell = \sum_{\substack{\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^k \\ |\alpha| = \ell}} a_\alpha \, \mathrm{sym}\!\left(\vec{w}^{\star\,\otimes\alpha_1}_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes \vec{w}^{\star\,\otimes\alpha_k}_k\right) \in (\mathbb{R}^d)^{\otimes \ell}.
\end{equation}
Exactly as in the single-index case~\eqref{eq:intro:drift-ie}, the escape from initialization (\(\vec{m} \approx 0\)) is governed by the leading nontrivial tensor. The population gradient drives the student weights along the contraction of \(T_\ell\) with \(\vec{w}\) itself,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:leap-drift}
    \dod{\vec{w}}{t} \;\propto\; \ell!\,b_\ell\, T_\ell[\vec{w}^{\otimes(\ell-1)}],
    \qquad \ell = \min\{|\alpha| : a_\alpha \neq 0\},
\end{equation}
and the overlap vector follows by projection onto the teacher directions,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:leap-drift-overlap}
    \dod{m_r}{t} = \frac{1}{d}\left\langle \vec{w}^\star_r,\, \dod{\vec{w}}{t} \right\rangle, \qquad r \in [k].
\end{equation}
Here \(b_\ell\) is the \(\ell\)-th Hermite coefficient of \(\sigma\), and \(T_\ell[\vec{w}^{\otimes(\ell-1)}] \in \mathbb{R}^d\) denotes the contraction of \(T_\ell\) with \((\ell-1)\) copies of \(\vec{w}\); each such contraction brings down a factor \(\langle \vec{w}^\star_s, \vec{w}\rangle = d\,m_s\), so the drift of \(\vec{m}\) is a polynomial of degree \(\ell-1\) in the overlaps---the multi-index counterpart of the \(m^{\ell-1}\) drift of~\eqref{eq:intro:drift-ie}. The integer \(\ell\) is the \emph{leap exponent} (or leap complexity) of \citet{abbe2022merged, abbe2023sgd}: the lowest degree of the non-trivial Hermite tensors of \(f^\star\).

An equivalent formulation, direction by direction, makes the analogy with the single-index case fully explicit. For each direction \(\vec{v}\) in the teacher subspace \(V^\star = \mathrm{span}(\vec{w}^\star_1,\ldots,\vec{w}^\star_k)\), define the \emph{directional information exponent} \citep{arnaboldi2024repetita}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:dir-info-exponent}
    \ell_{\vec{v}} = \min\!\left\{j \geq 1 : \mathbb{E}\!\left[y\,\mathrm{He}_j\!\left(\langle \vec{v}, \vec{x}\rangle\right)\right] \neq 0\right\};
\end{equation}
then the leap exponent is \(\ell = \min_{\vec{v} \in V^\star} \ell_{\vec{v}}\), and the direction achieving the minimum is the one the student aligns with first.

\paragraph{Staircase dynamics} Capturing the staircase dynamics requires the student to also have \(p \geq k\) neurons,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:intro:multi-neuron-student}
    f(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{p}\sum_{j=1}^p a_j\,\sigma\!\left(\frac{\vec{w}_j \cdot \vec{x}}{\sqrt{d}}\right),
\end{equation}
each with its own overlap vector \(\vec{m}_j = W^\star\vec{w}_j/d \in \mathbb{R}^k\). Every neuron starts from an independent random initialization and evolves according to Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:leap-drift}, so different neurons can specialize to different teacher directions.

Multi-index learning then unfolds as a \emph{staircase} \citep{abbe2021staircase}. At initialization all overlaps are \(\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{d})\), so all terms in the expansion~\eqref{eq:intro:multi-index-hermite} vanish and the dynamics is flat in every direction. Neurons first align with the direction \(\vec{v}\) achieving the minimum \(\ell_{\vec{v}} = \ell\), requiring \(\mathcal{O}(d^{\ell-1})\) samples. Once that direction is weakly recovered, its overlap \(m_r\) reaches an \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) value and henceforth acts as a constant multiplier: every coefficient \(a_\alpha\) with \(\alpha_r > 0\) in~\eqref{eq:intro:multi-index-hermite} now contributes a finite term to the correlation rather than vanishing. This lowers the effective directional information exponent of the remaining directions---the exponent that was \(\ell_{\vec{v}'}\) (minimum total degree with \(\alpha_{v'} > 0\)) becomes the minimum degree in direction \(\vec{v}'\) alone, which can be strictly smaller---and the next escape costs fewer samples. The dynamics progresses from saddle to saddle in turn. Targets all of whose directions are linearly connected (leap one) are recovered in \(\mathcal{O}(d)\) samples, while each additional unit of leap raises the cost in direct analogy with~\eqref{eq:intro:info-scaling}. 







\subsection{Generative exponent}

Returning to the single-index setting, the population risk on the sphere is \(\mathcal{R}(m) = \mathrm{const} - C(m)\) (Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:correlation-potential}). The gradient with respect to the student weights is therefore
\[
    \nabla_{\vec{w}}\mathcal{R} = -\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[y\,\sigma'(\lambda)\,\vec{x}\right],
\]
which is a \emph{correlational query}: a functional of the input that is \emph{linear} in the label \(y\). This is the fundamental constraint of one-pass SGD---each gradient step can only ask ``how correlated is \(y\) with this function of \(\vec{x}\)?''---and it is shared by every algorithm that processes each sample exactly once.

The class of algorithms restricted to queries of the form \(\mathbb{E}[y\,\varphi(\vec{x})]\) for arbitrary \(\varphi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}\) is called the \emph{Correlated Statistical Query} (CSQ) model. \citet{damian2023smoothing} established a CSQ lower bound: no CSQ algorithm can achieve weak recovery of a target with information exponent \(\ell\) with fewer than \(d^{\ell/2}\) samples. This bound is strictly tighter than the \(d^{\ell-1}\) rate of one-pass SGD for \(\ell \geq 3\); the gap between the two arises from the stochastic fluctuations that dominate the escape from the flat saddle, which loss smoothing suppresses. The CSQ bound \(d^{\ell/2}\) is tight: the \emph{partial-trace} algorithm of \citet{pmlr-v65-anandkumar17a} achieves it by directly estimating the population Hermite tensor \(T_\ell\) via a spectral method (see Appendix~\ref{app:intro:partial-trace}); \citet{damian2024computational} propose a gradient-based algorithm on a smoothed loss that achieves the same bound. However, it is still a CSQ algorithm---it correlates the raw label \(y\) linearly with a function of \(\vec{x}\), and therefore cannot break the CSQ barrier.

Both one-pass SGD and the partial-trace algorithm are restricted to querying the label in its raw form, without prior nonlinear transformation. The key question is: what if we first apply a nonlinear transformation \(g\) to the label, and then query \(\mathbb{E}[g(y)\,\varphi(\vec{x})]\)? The minimum Hermite degree for which such a query can be non-zero defines a new exponent. \citet{damian2024computational} formalized this as the \emph{generative exponent}:
\begin{definition}[Generative exponent \cite{damian2024computational}]
\begin{equation} \label{eq:intro:generative-exponent}
    \ell^\star = \min\left\{ j \geq 1 : \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)}\!\left[ g(h^\star(\xi))\,\mathrm{He}_j(\xi) \right] \neq 0 \ \text{ for some } g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \right\},
\end{equation}
where the minimization now ranges over all label transformations \(g\) as well as over the Hermite degree.
\end{definition}
Since the choice \(g = \mathrm{id}\) recovers Equation~\eqref{eq:intro:info-exponent}, one always has \(\ell^\star \leq \ell\); a nonlinear \(g\) can only lower the exponent, and frequently does so strictly.
This gap can be substantial, as illustrated in the following examples.
\begin{example}[\(\mathrm{He}_3\)]
 \(\mathrm{He}_3\) has information exponent \(\ell = 3\), yet because it is odd one can take \(g(y) = \mathrm{sign}(y)\) and correlate the transformed label with \(\mathrm{He}_1\), giving \(\ell^\star = 1\).
 From a practical standpoint, this means that even the optimal partial-trace algorithm recovers \(\vec{w}\) in \(\mathcal{O}(d^{3/2})\) when restricted to linear queries of the label, but if it is allowed to first transform the label with \(g(y) = \mathrm{sign}(y)\) it can recover \(\vec{w}\) in \(\mathcal{O}(d)\). See Figure~\ref{fig:intro:gen-exp-H3} for a numerical illustration.
\end{example}
\begin{example}[\(\mathrm{He}_4\)]
 \(\mathrm{He}_4\) has information exponent \(\ell = 4\), but taking \(g(y) = y^2\) allows one to correlate the transformed label with \(\mathrm{He}_2\), giving \(\ell^\star = 2\). The complexity drops from \(\mathcal{O}(d^2)\) under CSQ to \(\mathcal{O}(d\log d)\); see Figure~\ref{fig:intro:gen-exp-H4} for a numerical illustration.
\end{example}
The generative exponent governs the \emph{Statistical Query} (SQ) lower bound \(d^{\ell^\star/2}\); problems with \(\ell^\star > 2\) are genuinely hard: even the best first-order method, approximate message passing, fails on them \citep{troiani2024fundamental}.

\paragraph{Displaced gradient SGD achieves the generative exponent rate}
The generative exponent sets the complexity lower bound, but can a gradient-based algorithm achieve it \emph{agnostically}, without prior knowledge of the optimal transformation \(g\)? The key mechanism is remarkably simple: \emph{data reuse}. When the gradient step uses the same sample twice, as in the displaced gradient algorithm of Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2405}, the update direction becomes a nonlinear function of \(y\). Concretely, the displaced gradient first computes a perturbed weight \(\tilde{\vec{w}} = \vec{w} - \rho\,\nabla_{\vec{w}}\ell\) and then evaluates the gradient at \(\tilde{\vec{w}}\) on the same sample: \addnote{If \(\rho\) is positive the method is known as \emph{extragradient}, and if \(\rho\) is negative it is known as \emph{sharpness-aware minimization}.}
\[
    \nabla_{\vec{w}}\ell\!\left(f(\vec{x};\tilde{\vec{w}}),\,y\right) = -\,y\,\sigma'\!\big(\langle\tilde{\vec{w}},\vec{x}\rangle\big)\,\vec{x}.
\]
With \(\rho \sim 1/d\), the inner product \(\langle\tilde{\vec{w}},\vec{x}\rangle = \langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle + \rho\,y\,\sigma'(\langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle)\,\|\vec{x}\|^2\) receives an \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) perturbation that is a \emph{nonlinear} function of \(y\) (because \(\|\vec{x}\|^2 \approx d\) and the product \(\rho d\) yields an \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) contribution). Taylor-expanding \(\sigma'\) around \(\langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle\) generates higher-order correlational queries \(\mathbb{E}[p(y)\,\varphi(\vec{x})]\) for a polynomial \(p\) determined by the activation
\[
    \nabla_{\vec{w}}\ell\!\left(f(\vec{x};\tilde{\vec{w}}),\,y\right) = -y \left(\sigma'(\langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle) + \,y\,\sigma''(\langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle) + y^2\,\sigma'''(\langle\vec{w},\vec{x}\rangle) + \cdots \right)\,\vec{x},
\] so the network implicitly synthesizes the label transformation, provided \(\sigma\) has higher-order derivatives.
This result is formalized in the following theorem:
\begin{theorem}[Informal version of Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2405}]
    The displaced gradient algorithm of Chapter~\ref{chap:paper-2405}, which sees each sample exactly twice, achieves weak recovery of any polynomial single-index target with generative exponent \(\ell^\star\) in
    \begin{equation}
        n = \begin{cases}
            \mathcal{O}(d) & \ell^\star = 1, \\
            \mathcal{O}(d\log d) & \ell^\star = 2.
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{theorem}
This is the same sample-complexity table as~\eqref{eq:intro:sgd-sample-complexity}, with \(\ell\) replaced by \(\ell^\star\). Since \(\ell^\star \leq \ell\), the displaced gradient is always at least as efficient as one-pass SGD and strictly better whenever \(\ell^\star < \ell\).

In the multi-index setting, data reuse extends the staircase hierarchy. One-pass SGD follows the \emph{CSQ staircase} of \citet{abbe2021staircase}: each phase aligns with the direction of smallest \emph{directional information exponent} and the cost of each step scales as \(d^{\ell_{\vec{v}}-1}\). With the displaced gradient, the staircase is governed by the generative exponent instead, enabling an \emph{SQ staircase} over a strictly larger class of functions. A canonical example is \(h^\star(\vec{z}) = \mathrm{He}_4(z_1) + \mathrm{sign}(z_1 z_2 z_3)\): the \(\mathrm{He}_4\) component has \(\ell = 4\), \(\ell^\star = 2\), and the displaced gradient weakly recovers the first direction in \(\mathcal{O}(d\log d)\) steps; once \(m_1 = \mathcal{O}(1)\), the \(\mathrm{sign}\) component, which has \(\ell=\ell^\star = 3\), becomes learnable and is recovered in a second \(\mathcal{O}(d\log d)\) phase. The same function is invisible to the CSQ staircase because its first direction has \(\ell = 4\).

Together, the three exponents trace the arc of this thesis: the information exponent fixes the cost of one-pass SGD on a single-index target, the leap exponent lifts it to the multi-index staircase, and the generative exponent marks the lower limit that becomes reachable once we step beyond a single pass.
